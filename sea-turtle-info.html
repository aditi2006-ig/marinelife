SNAPPING TURTLE

The snapping turtle (Chelydra serpentina) is a robust freshwater turtle native to North America, easily identified by its large, powerful body and distinctive long tail. Its shell is low and flattened, often dark brown or olive, and it frequently sports a coat of algae for camouflage in its aquatic habitats. Snapping turtles are primarily found in rivers, lakes, ponds, and marshes, where they excel at swimming and often remain hidden in the mud, waiting for prey. Their diet consists of aquatic plants, fish, insects, and small mammals, and they are known for their strong, beak-like jaws that can deliver a painful bite when threatened. Reproduction occurs in spring and summer when females travel to dry land to lay eggs in sandy soil, depositing between 20 to 50 eggs that hatch after 75 to 90 days. Snapping turtles can live for several decades, playing a crucial role in their ecosystems as both predators and scavengers, helping maintain the balance of aquatic life. However, they face threats from habitat destruction, pollution, and illegal hunting. Conservation efforts are essential to protect this unique species and ensure the health of the freshwater environments they inhabit.

  • They can live up to a maximum age over 100 years
  • The common snapping turtle uses its large claws for digging, not offense – though the claws can become dangerous when handled by humans.

SPOTTED TURTLE

The spotted turtle (Clemmys guttata) is a small, distinctive freshwater turtle native to the eastern United States and parts of Canada. Recognized for its striking appearance, the species features a dark, smooth shell adorned with bright yellow or white spots, which vary in size and pattern among individuals. Typically growing to about 4 to 5.5 inches in length, spotted turtles inhabit shallow, slow-moving waters such as ponds, marshes, and wetlands, where they often bask on logs or rocks. Spotted turtles are primarily omnivorous, feeding on a diet that includes aquatic plants, insects, and small invertebrates. They are known for their shy and secretive nature, often retreating into the water when approached. Unfortunately, this charming species faces numerous threats, including habitat loss, pollution, and the illegal pet trade, leading to a decline in populations. Conservation efforts are essential to protect their habitats and ensure the survival of this unique and beautiful turtle..

  • They can live up 65 to 110 years.
  • Carapace is keelless and contains a varying number of yellow spots.

EASTERN BOX TURTLE

The eastern box turtle (Terrapene carolina carolina) is a well-known terrestrial turtle native to the eastern United States. Recognized for its distinctive dome-shaped shell, which can vary in color from brown to olive with bright yellow or orange markings, this species typically measures 4.5 to 6 inches in length. The shell is hinged, allowing the turtle to completely close itself inside for protection against predators, a unique feature among turtles. Eastern box turtles inhabit a variety of environments, including forests, grasslands, and wetlands, where they thrive in humid conditions. These turtles are omnivorous, feeding on a diverse diet that includes fruits, vegetables, insects, and small invertebrates. Eastern box turtles are known for their long lifespans, often living over 50 years in the wild, with some individuals reported to exceed 100 years. However, they face significant threats from habitat destruction, road mortality, and the pet trade, leading to declining populations in many areas. Conservation efforts are crucial to protect their habitats and ensure the survival of this iconic species..

  • They can live up to a maximum age over 100 years
  • But in wilderness,they generally live for 25 to 35 years.

RED EARED Turtle

The red-eared slider (Trachemys scripta elegans) is a popular freshwater turtle native to the southern United States but has also become established in various regions worldwide due to the pet trade. Easily recognized by the distinctive red stripe behind each eye, this turtle features a smooth, oval-shaped shell that can reach lengths of up to 12 inches. Their coloration typically includes a dark green or brown shell with yellow markings, providing excellent camouflage in their natural habitats, which include ponds, lakes, and slow-moving rivers. Red-eared sliders are omnivorous, feeding on a varied diet that includes aquatic plants, insects, small fish, and crustaceans. They are known for their adaptability and social behavior, often basking in groups on logs or rocks to absorb sunlight. However, these turtles face threats from habitat loss, pollution, and competition with native species. Conservation efforts are important to manage populations and protect their natural habitats, ensuring that red-eared sliders continue to thrive in both their native and adopted environments..

  • They can live up to 20-30 years.
  • In captivity,they can live over 40 years.

MISSISSIPPI MAP TURTLE

The Mississippi map turtle (Graptemys pseudogeographica kohnii) is a medium-sized freshwater turtle native to the southeastern United States, particularly in the Mississippi River basin. Recognizable by its distinctive shell pattern, the turtle features a dark olive or brown carapace marked with intricate yellow or cream-colored lines that resemble a map, giving the species its name. Adults typically reach sizes of 5 to 10 inches in length and are often found in slow-moving rivers, lakes, and backwaters, where they bask on logs and rocks. Mississippi map turtles are primarily omnivorous, feeding on a diet that includes aquatic plants, invertebrates, and small fish. They exhibit interesting social behaviors, often basking in groups, but can be quite shy and quick to retreat into the water when approached. The species faces threats from habitat loss, pollution, and illegal collection for the pet trade, leading to concerns about its conservation status. Efforts to protect their habitats and promote awareness are essential to ensure the continued survival of this unique and beautiful turtle.

  • They can live up to a maximum age of 30 years
  • The gray carapace is covered in yellow lines which resemble contour lines of a map

EASTERN LONG-NECKED TURTLE

The eastern long-necked turtle (Chelodina longicollis) is a distinctive freshwater turtle native to eastern Australia, particularly in rivers, lakes, and swamps. Known for its remarkably elongated neck, which can be as long as its shell, this species allows it to reach underwater vegetation and prey without exposing much of its body to potential threats. The shell is typically dark brown or olive with a smooth surface, and the turtle usually grows to a size of about 12 to 16 inches in length. Eastern long-necked turtles are primarily carnivorous, feeding on a diet that includes aquatic invertebrates, fish, and crustaceans. They are adept swimmers, using their long necks to maneuver effectively in the water. While they are generally shy, they can often be found basking on logs or rocks. However, their populations are threatened by habitat degradation, pollution, and the impacts of climate change. Conservation efforts are vital to protect their natural habitats and ensure the survival of this unique and intriguing species.

  • Scientific name: Chelodina longicollis Suborder: Pleurodira (side-necked turtles)
  • Where found: Australia

DESERT TORTOISE

The desert tortoise (Gopherus agassizii) is a remarkable species native to the arid regions of the southwestern United States and parts of Mexico. Adapted to harsh desert environments, this tortoise has a high-domed shell that can reach lengths of 10 to 14 inches, typically featuring a brown to tan coloration that helps it blend into its surroundings. It plays a crucial role in its ecosystem by digging burrows that provide shelter not only for itself but also for other desert wildlife. Desert tortoises are herbivorous, primarily feeding on a diet of native grasses, wildflowers, and various desert plants. They are well-known for their ability to conserve water, enabling them to survive in extreme heat and dry conditions. However, their populations have been declining due to habitat loss, human encroachment, and disease. Conservation efforts are essential to protect this iconic species and its fragile desert habitat, ensuring the survival of the desert tortoise for future generations.

  • They can live up to a maximum age of 35-80 years
  • They are critically endanngered species.

SOFTSHELL TURTLE

Softshell turtles, belonging to the family Trionychidae, are a diverse group of freshwater turtles recognized for their unique, flexible shells that lack the hard, bony structure typical of other turtles. Instead, their shells are covered by a layer of soft, leathery skin, which provides them with a streamlined shape ideal for swimming. Softshell turtles can be found in various habitats, including rivers, lakes, and ponds across North America, Asia, and Africa. They typically have long necks and flat bodies, allowing them to hide in sandy or muddy substrates while foraging for food. These turtles are primarily carnivorous, feeding on a diet that includes fish, amphibians, and invertebrates. Their ambush hunting style involves lying partially buried in the substrate, using their long necks to strike at passing prey. Softshell turtles are known for their speed both in water and on land, making them effective predators. Despite their adaptability, they face threats from habitat destruction, pollution, and overharvesting for the pet trade and food markets. Conservation efforts are crucial to ensure the survival of these fascinating and unique turtles in their natural environments.

  • They can live up to 100 years.
  • Scientific name: Apalone ferox
