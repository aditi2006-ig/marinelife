CARIBBEAN MONK SEAL

INTRODUCTION

The Caribbean monk seal (Neomonachus tropicalis) was a robust seal species native to the Caribbean Sea and Gulf of Mexico, characterized by its stout body, short snout, and dark brown to grayish fur. Adults typically weighed between 400 and 600 pounds and measured about 7 to 8 feet in length, often found resting on sandy beaches or rocky shores. Unfortunately, human activities such as hunting and habitat destruction led to a significant decline in their population, resulting in their extinction by 2008. The loss of the Caribbean monk seal serves as a poignant reminder of the urgent need for conservation efforts to protect marine ecosystems and the diverse species that inhabit them.

CAUSE OF DECLINE

The decline of the Caribbean monk seal was primarily due to human activities. Overhunting for their blubber and skin in the 18th and 19th centuries severely reduced their population. Additionally, habitat destruction from coastal development, pollution, and fishing activities further impacted their breeding and foraging grounds. Competition with other marine species for food, coupled with the effects of climate change, exacerbated their vulnerability. These combined factors ultimately led to their extinction by 2008, highlighting the critical need for sustainable practices to protect marine life.
