INTRODUCTION

The baiji, or Yangtze river dolphin (Lipotes vexillifer), is a critically endangered freshwater cetacean native to the Yangtze River in China. Known for its distinctively long and narrow snout, the baiji was once a common sight in the river's waters, but its population has drastically declined due to habitat loss, pollution, and increased boat traffic. Despite extensive conservation efforts, the baiji is now believed to be functionally extinct, with the last confirmed sighting occurring in the early 2000s. This loss serves as a stark reminder of the fragility of aquatic ecosystems and the urgent need for effective conservation strategies to protect other endangered species.

CAUSES OF DECLINE The decline of the baiji, or Yangtze river dolphin, is primarily due to habitat loss from dam construction, pollution degrading water quality, and overfishing that reduces food availability. Increased boat traffic leads to collisions and noise pollution, further disrupting their environment. Climate change also impacts their habitat, contributing to the species' dramatic decline and functional extinction.
