Sea Bed Grass

Seagrass beds are underwater ecosystems formed by marine flowering plants that grow in shallow coastal waters. Unlike seaweed, seagrasses have roots, stems, and leaves, making them more similar to land plants. These beds play a crucial role in maintaining marine biodiversity by providing habitat and food for various marine species, including fish, sea turtles, and dugongs. One fascinating fact about seagrass beds is that they produce oxygen even underwater, supporting marine life in oxygen-poor environments. They are among the most productive ecosystems, capable of storing carbon up to 35 times faster than tropical rainforests. Some seagrass meadows are thousands of years old, making them one of the longest-living plant communities on Earth. However, these vital ecosystems are under threat due to pollution, coastal development, and climate change, highlighting the need for conservation efforts to protect them.
