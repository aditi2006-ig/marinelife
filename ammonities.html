AMMONITES

CAUSE OF DECLINE

INRODUCTION

Ammonites are an extinct group of marine mollusks that thrived during the Mesozoic Era, particularly from the Late Cambrian to the Cretaceous period. Characterized by their coiled and chambered shells, ammonites are closely related to modern cephalopods, such as squids and octopuses. These creatures played a significant role in ancient marine ecosystems, serving as both predators and prey. Fossils of ammonites are widely studied by paleontologists, as their distinct shell shapes and intricate suture patterns allow for precise dating of rock layers, providing valuable insights into the Earth's geological history and the evolution of life in the oceans..

Ammonites declined primarily due to the mass extinction event at the end of the Cretaceous period, likely caused by a massive asteroid impact that led to drastic environmental changes, including climate shifts and disrupted food chains. Additionally, volcanic activity from the Deccan Traps contributed to further ecological stress through greenhouse gas emissions and ocean acidification. These factors collectively resulted in the extinction of ammonites and many other marine species.

Where Ammonites Are Found

Ammonite fossils can be found all over the world, in many types of marine sedimentary rocks: Great Plains of North America Himalaya foothills Antarctica glaciers River valleys in north India, such as Spiti and Ladakh

Did you know?Ammonites were born with tiny shells and, as they grew, they built new chambers onto it. They would move their entire body into a new chamber and seal off their old and now too-small living quarters with walls known as septa.
