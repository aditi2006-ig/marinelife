JAPENESE SEA LION




The Japanese sea lion, or "Nihoniruka," was a distinct marine mammal native to the coastal waters of Japan. Recognized for its unique physical features, including a robust body and a mane-like layer of fur in males, it thrived in the warm waters of the region. Sadly, the species was declared extinct in the 1970s due to overhunting, habitat loss, and competition with fisheries. Efforts to raise awareness about this species have highlighted the importance of marine conservation and the need to protect other marine life from similar fates.

CAUSES OF DECLINE
The decline of the Japanese sea lion was primarily caused by overhunting for its fur, meat, and oil. Habitat destruction due to coastal development and pollution also played significant roles. Additionally, competition with fisheries for food resources and entanglement in fishing gear further contributed to their decline. These combined pressures led to a drastic reduction in their population, ultimately resulting in their extinction.

Where Japenese sea lion Are Found

"The Japanese sea lion, also known as the black sea lion, lived in the Northwest Pacific, visiting the maritime area of Japan, Korea and Russian Kamchatka Island. They belonged to the eared seals, and in contrast to true seals, they could turn their hind flippers to face forward, giving them greater mobility. Japanese sea lions had no natural predators, so the main cause of their extinction were humans. They were hunted for their fat (to make oil lamps), their meat, their skins and even their whiskers (used to make pipe cleaners). Fishermen killed them, to have more fish to themselves.
